Uxaria Web Interface Toolkit

Overview

Uxaria is a web interface toolkit based on Twitter Bootstrap. It shares the naming conventions for classes and variables with Bootstrap, but differs in design and implementation.

Uxaria provides a modular set of sane default styles for most common elements in complex web designs. Responsive design is at the core of this project, with the flexibilty for adaptions that make sense in mind.

Basic Styles

Font

The default font size is set to 0.9375em – corresponding to 15px in most browsers. This font size is used for mobile phones up to 568px, for tablets in portrait orientation with 768px device width and for tablets and small desktops with 1024px device width.

For larger screens the font size is adjusted to fill the device or browser window width up to a maximum of 1.25em (corresponding to 20px) on a 1366px wide screen.

The default line height is 1.4 - a proven value for good readability for lines with approximately 80 characters per line. The grid system is optimized for this line length at 6 grid columns.

Uxaria includes the marvelous Source Sans Pro font family by Adobe via Google Fonts. This clean, web-optimzed and open source sans-serif font makes a good default and comes in light, regular and bold font weights.

Color

Uxaria sets the following default colors:

  • Background: #fff
  • Text: #222
  • Links: #3a6bd3, hover: #0f51b3
  • Selection: #afe3fd

Grid

Regular Grid

By default Uxaria uses a 12-column grid system. Grid dimensions are in em, making them scale proportionally with the base font size. The default grid gutter is 1.4em - equal to the line height by design.

Responsive Grid

Instead of shrinking / widening column and gutter widths in responsive design (like Bootstrap and most other frameworks do), Uxaria reduces the number of colums available. On tablet in portrait orientation each row consists of 9 grid columns. This is further reduced to 6 grid colmns on mobile phones in landscape orientation. For mobile phones in portrait orientation we finally stack the columns vertically.

The layout breakpoints are:

Phone portrait: up to 511px
all columns stacked vertically
Phone landscape: from 512px to 749px
6 columns
Tablet portrait: from 750px to 1023px
9 columns
Tablet landscape and desktop: 1024px and up
12 columns

The motivation behind this design decision is to give you as a web designer fine-grained control over responsive layout mechanics while keeping all proportions within content elements as constant as possible on all devices. Assign the helper classes span*-phone and span*-tablet to rearrange elements for smaller screens.

Dimensions

The Extremes of Good and EvilThe Mistaken Idea of Denouncing Pleasure

Cicero

But I must explain to you how all this mistaken idea of denouncing pleasure and praising pain was born and I will give you a complete account of the system, and expound the actual teachings of the great explorer of the truth, the master-builder of human happiness.

Pleasure & Pain

No one rejects, dislikes, or avoids pleasure itself, because it is pleasure, but because those who do not know how to pursue pleasure rationally encounter consequences that are extremely painful. Nor again is there anyone who loves or pursues or desires to obtain pain of itself, because it is pain, but because occasionally circumstances occur in which toil and pain can procure him some great pleasure. To take a trivial example, which of us ever undertakes laborious physical exercise, except to obtain some advantage from it? But who has any right to find fault with a man who chooses to enjoy a pleasure that has no annoying consequences, or one who avoids a pain that produces no resultant pleasure?

Weakness of Will

On the other hand, we denounce with righteous indignation and dislike men who are so beguiled and demoralized by the charms of pleasure of the moment, so blinded by desire, that they cannot foresee the pain and trouble that are bound to ensue; and equal blame belongs to those who fail in their duty through weakness of will, which is the same as saying through shrinking from toil and pain. These cases are perfectly simple and easy to distinguish. In a free hour, when our power of choice is untrammelled and when nothing prevents our being able to do what we like best, every pleasure is to be welcomed and every pain avoided. But in certain circumstances and owing to the claims of duty or the obligations of business is will frequently occur that pleasures have to be repudiated and annoyances accepted. The wise man therefore always holds in these matters to this principle of selection: he rejects pleasures to secure other greater pleasures, or else he endures pains to avoid worse pains.
